Laws & Meanings

A comprehensive repository of physical laws, principles, and mathematical foundations for high school physics.

Classical Mechanics

Newton's Second Law

F = m · a

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

F: Net force (N) m: Mass (kg) a: Acceleration (m/s²)

Law of Universal Gravitation

F = G · (m₁m₂) / r²

Every point mass attracts every other point mass by a force acting along the line intersecting both points.

G: Gravitational constant m₁, m₂: Masses (kg) r: Distance (m)

Work-Energy Theorem

W = ΔK

The work done by the net force on a particle equals the change in its kinetic energy.

W: Work (J) ΔK: Change in Kinetic Energy (J)

Conservation of Momentum

p_initial = p_final

In an isolated system, the total momentum remains constant during a collision or interaction.

p: Momentum (kg·m/s) p = m · v

Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics

ΔU = Q - W

The change in internal energy of a system is equal to the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system.

ΔU: Internal energy change (J) Q: Heat added (J) W: Work done (J)

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

The state of an amount of gas is determined by its pressure, volume, and temperature.

P: Pressure (Pa) V: Volume (m³) n: Amount of substance (mol) R: Ideal gas constant T: Temperature (K)

Fourier's Law (Heat Conduction)

q = -k · ∇T

The time rate of heat transfer through a material is proportional to the negative gradient in the temperature.

q: Local heat flux density (W/m²) k: Thermal conductivity (W/m·K) ∇T: Temperature gradient (K/m)

Electricity & Magnetism

Ohm's Law

V = I · R

The current through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the voltage across the two points.

V: Voltage (V) I: Current (A) R: Resistance (Ω)

Coulomb's Law

F = k · (q₁q₂) / r²

The force between two point charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

F: Electric force (N) q₁, q₂: Charges (C) k: Coulomb's constant r: Distance (m)

Faraday's Law

ε = -dΦ / dt

Any change in the magnetic environment of a coil of wire will cause a voltage (EMF) to be induced in the coil.

ε: Electromotive force (V) Φ: Magnetic flux (Wb) t: Time (s)

Optics & Waves

Snell's Law

n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂

Describes the relationship between the angles of incidence and refraction when light passes through the boundary between two different media.

n₁, n₂: Refractive indices θ₁, θ₂: Angles of incidence/refraction

Wave Speed Equation

v = f · λ

The speed of a wave is equal to its frequency multiplied by its wavelength.

v: Wave speed (m/s) f: Frequency (Hz) λ: Wavelength (m)

Law of Reflection

θ_i = θ_r

The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection when a wave reflects off a surface.

Modern Physics

Einstein's Mass-Energy Equivalence

E = mc²

Energy and mass are different forms of the same thing; a small amount of mass can be converted into a large amount of energy.

E: Energy (J) m: Mass (kg) c: Speed of light (≈ 3·10⁸ m/s)

Planck's Relation

E = h · f

The energy of a photon is proportional to its frequency.

E: Photon energy (J) h: Planck constant f: Frequency (Hz)

De Broglie Wavelength

λ = h / p

Matter exhibits wave-like behavior; the wavelength is inversely proportional to the momentum.

λ: Wavelength (m) h: Planck constant p: Momentum (kg·m/s)
